01. Functions

Goals

By the end of this session, students should be able to:

  • describe functions verbally, numerically, visually, and algebraically
  • find domains, ranges, and graphs of functions of one real variable
  • work with piecewise functions, composite functions, one-to-one functions, and inverse functions
  • recognize periodicity, monotonicity, symmetry, and boundedness
  • know the basic elementary families: linear, polynomial, power, exponential, logarithmic, trigonometric, inverse trigonometric, and hyperbolic functions
  • use shifts, stretches, shrinks, reflections, absolute values, and \(f(|x|)\) to build new graphs from old graphs

Function Review Checklist

By the end of the function review, students should be able to do the following.

Functions and Graphs

  • Give examples of functions from formulas, tables, descriptions, and graphs.
  • State what it means for \(f:X\to E\) to be a function: each input \(x\in X\) has exactly one output \(f(x)\in E\).
  • Identify the independent variable, dependent variable, domain \(D(f)\), and range \(E(f)\).
  • Interpret the graph of \(f\) as the set of points \((x,f(x))\).
  • Find the domain and range of common formulas, including square-root expressions.
  • Evaluate and sketch piecewise-defined functions.
  • Compute compositions:

\[ (f\circ g)(x)=f(g(x)). \]

  • Decide whether a function is one-to-one and find inverse functions when possible.

Basic Properties of Functions

  • Recognize periodic functions and use the identity

\[ f(x+T)=f(x) \]

for a period \(T>0\).

  • Determine whether a function is increasing or decreasing on an interval.
  • Test symmetry:

\[ f(-x)=f(x) \quad \text{even}, \qquad f(-x)=-f(x) \quad \text{odd}. \]

  • Decide whether a function is bounded above, bounded below, bounded, or unbounded on a given domain.

Elementary Functions

  • Linear functions: know \(y=mx+b\), interpret \(m\) as slope, and find equations of lines.
  • Polynomials: recognize degree, leading coefficient, and common linear/quadratic/cubic cases.
  • Power functions: know how \(y=x^\alpha\) changes with \(\alpha\), including \(x^2\), \(1/x\), and \(\sqrt{x}\).
  • Exponential functions: know \(y=a^x\) for \(a>0\), \(a\neq 1\), their domains, ranges, monotonicity, and laws of exponents.
  • Logarithmic functions: know \(y=\log_a x\) as the inverse of \(y=a^x\), its domain/range, monotonicity, and log laws.
  • Trigonometric functions: know the basic properties of \(\sin x\), \(\cos x\), \(\tan x\), and \(\cot x\), including domains, ranges, periods, symmetry, and basic identities.
  • Inverse trigonometric functions: know the defining restrictions for \(\arcsin x\), \(\arccos x\), and \(\arctan x\).
  • Hyperbolic functions: know the definitions of \(\sinh x\), \(\cosh x\), \(\tanh x\), and \(\coth x\), plus the basic identity

\[ \cosh^2 x-\sinh^2 x=1. \]

Transformations

Starting from the graph of \(y=f(x)\), students should know how to graph:

  • \(y=f(x)+c\) and \(y=f(x)-c\) by vertical shifts
  • \(y=f(x+c)\) and \(y=f(x-c)\) by horizontal shifts
  • \(y=cf(x)\) and \(y=\frac{1}{c}f(x)\) by vertical stretch/shrink
  • \(y=f(cx)\) and \(y=f(x/c)\) by horizontal shrink/stretch
  • \(y=-f(x)\) and \(y=f(-x)\) by reflections
  • \(y=|f(x)|\) and \(y=f(|x|)\) by reflecting selected parts of the graph

Active Prelude to Calculus Summary

Active Prelude to Calculus is a pre-calculus bridge text by Matthew Boelkins. For this course, it is most useful as the reading source for function review before limits and derivatives.

The book is organized around five main blocks:

  • Relating Changing Quantities: functions as models, average rate of change, linear functions, quadratic functions, composition, inverse functions, transformations, combining functions, and piecewise functions.
  • Circular Functions: the unit circle, sine and cosine, radians, sinusoidal functions, and periodic behavior.
  • Exponential and Logarithmic Functions: exponential growth and decay, modeling, the number \(e\), logarithms, log properties, and applications such as temperature and population.
  • Trigonometry: right-triangle trigonometry, tangent, inverse trigonometric functions, angle-finding, secant/cosecant/cotangent, and basic identities.
  • Polynomial and Rational Functions: power functions, polynomial behavior, zeros and signs, polynomial modeling, rational functions, domains, holes, and long-range behavior.

Active Prelude Chapters 1-5 support most of the function review. Two topics need extra treatment in these course notes: boundedness as a formal property of functions and hyperbolic functions.

Core Examples

A function is a rule that assigns each input exactly one output. In modeling, the hardest part is often not evaluating a formula, but deciding what the input and output should represent.

For functions \(f\) and \(g\), the composition

\[ (f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x)) \]

means that \(g\) acts first and \(f\) acts second.

Warm-Up

Suppose the temperature of a metal plate at time \(t\) is \(T(t)\), and the resistance of a sensor at temperature \(T\) is \(R(T)\).

  1. What does \(R(T(t))\) represent?
  2. What is the input variable?
  3. What units should the output have?

Example

Let

\[ f(x)=\sqrt{x+4}, \qquad g(x)=x^2-1. \]

Then

\[ (f \circ g)(x)=f(x^2-1)=\sqrt{x^2+3}. \]

The formula is defined for all real \(x\), because \(x^2+3>0\).

Hyperbolic Functions

Hyperbolic functions are built from exponential functions. They appear naturally in differential equations, geometry, and several engineering models.

Definitions

The hyperbolic sine and hyperbolic cosine are defined by

\[ \sinh x=\frac{e^x-e^{-x}}{2}, \qquad \cosh x=\frac{e^x+e^{-x}}{2}. \]

The hyperbolic tangent and hyperbolic cotangent are defined by

\[ \tanh x=\frac{\sinh x}{\cosh x} =\frac{e^x-e^{-x}}{e^x+e^{-x}}, \]

and

\[ \coth x=\frac{\cosh x}{\sinh x} =\frac{e^x+e^{-x}}{e^x-e^{-x}}, \qquad x\neq 0. \]

Basic Values

From the definitions,

\[ \sinh 0=0,\qquad \cosh 0=1,\qquad \tanh 0=0. \]

The function \(\coth x\) is not defined at \(x=0\), since \(\sinh 0=0\).

Domain, Range, and Shape

Function Domain Range Symmetry Basic behavior
\(\sinh x\) \(\mathbb{R}\) \(\mathbb{R}\) odd increasing on \(\mathbb{R}\)
\(\cosh x\) \(\mathbb{R}\) \([1,\infty)\) even decreases on \((-\infty,0)\), increases on \((0,\infty)\)
\(\tanh x\) \(\mathbb{R}\) \((-1,1)\) odd increasing on \(\mathbb{R}\)
\(\coth x\) \(\mathbb{R}\setminus\{0\}\) \((-\infty,-1)\cup(1,\infty)\) odd undefined at \(0\); horizontal asymptotes \(y=\pm 1\)

Identities

The most important identity is

\[ \cosh^2 x-\sinh^2 x=1. \]

This follows directly from the definitions:

\[ \cosh^2 x-\sinh^2 x =\left(\frac{e^x+e^{-x}}{2}\right)^2 -\left(\frac{e^x-e^{-x}}{2}\right)^2 =1. \]

Other useful identities include

\[ \sinh(-x)=-\sinh x,\qquad \cosh(-x)=\cosh x, \]

\[ \sinh(x+y)=\sinh x\cosh y+\cosh x\sinh y, \]

and

\[ \cosh(x+y)=\cosh x\cosh y+\sinh x\sinh y. \]

Example

Simplify

\[ \cosh^2 x-\tanh^2 x\cosh^2 x. \]

Since \(\tanh x=\sinh x/\cosh x\),

\[ \tanh^2 x\cosh^2 x=\sinh^2 x. \]

Therefore,

\[ \cosh^2 x-\tanh^2 x\cosh^2 x =\cosh^2 x-\sinh^2 x =1. \]
